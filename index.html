About the Project

Student-built electric race car

LEONIX is more than a car – it’s a full engineering journey. Students design the chassis, calculate gear ratios, wire the electrical system, and capture performance data during practice and race day. The project connects classroom physics, electronics, and programming with real competition pressure on track.

The goal is simple: build a safe, efficient, and competitive EVGP car that can complete the endurance race with the best possible lap consistency, battery strategy, and driver performance.

Technical Specs

Built for endurance & control

LEONIX is designed around a high-efficiency electric powertrain, lightweight chassis, and carefully tuned gearing. Below are placeholder specs you can update with final values once the car is fully tested.

Powertrain
Brushless DC Motor

High-torque brushless motor tuned for endurance racing. Powered by a 48V battery system with real-time current and voltage monitoring.

Battery
48 V Pack

Custom battery configuration optimized for race duration, balancing energy capacity with weight and safety constraints.

Performance
Target 33–35 km/h

Speed is tuned for stable cornering and efficient energy use, not just maximum straight-line speed.

Chassis & Body
Lightweight Frame

Student-designed chassis with safety roll hoops, driver ergonomics, and aerodynamic body inspired by karting and formula-style cars.

Safety Systems
Kill Switch & Fusing

External kill switch, main fuse protection, driver harness, and braking system aligned with competition rules.

Data & Strategy
Telemetry Driven

Race decisions are supported by live data using python Dashboard: speed, current, voltage, motor temperature, and energy used per lap.

Telemetry & AI

From track data to strategy

LEONIX uses a Cycle Analyst paired with Data logger and LoRa transmitter to send real-time performance data from the car to the pit. Voltage, current, speed, and amp-hours are monitored lap by lap, allowing the team to adjust driving style and pit strategy dynamically.

In the next phase, this data can feed an AI model that recommends target lap times, maximum current draw, and optimal pace to finish the race with the best possible position while respecting battery limits.
